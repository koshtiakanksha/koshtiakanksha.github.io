About

I am Akanksha. Here is how it all came together.

Math was my first love. I never had to study it twice. Puzzles, quizzes, sudoku — they just made sense. It was the one thing that felt both fun and effortless. That early joy of solving things never really left me.

When I found statistics, it felt like math with a purpose. It gave shape to ideas, turned questions into structure, and opened the door to understanding the world in numbers. I followed that path through my undergrad and master's, learning how things work and why they matter.

Somewhere along the way, I met code. What started as a course requirement quickly became something more. I saw how a few lines could replace hours of manual effort and scale beyond anything I could do by hand. It was not just faster, it was liberating.

That curiosity pulled me deeper into tech. I taught myself new tools, worked on side projects, and started building solutions from scratch. The blend of statistics and technology gave me the confidence to take on real data problems, and the clarity to see where I could make a difference.

I realized that among all the fields where data science could be applied, I was drawn to one in particular — helping businesses grow and helping people work better. That is what led me to Information Systems, and it is what continues to shape the kind of work I care about.

Then came Accessible AI. The first time I stood in front of a panel to pitch it, I felt everything I had learned up to that point come together. It was no longer about numbers or models. It was about people. Vision. Communication. And the ability to lead an idea forward.

That moment changed something for me. It showed me that building is not just technical. It is personal. It takes clarity, direction, and the courage to bring others along. That is why I chose to pursue an MBA, to build those muscles the same way I once built code and logic.

And somehow, it still feels like solving a puzzle. Different pieces, new challenges, but the same joy I felt when I first cracked open a math book. The circle feels complete. And I am just getting started.
